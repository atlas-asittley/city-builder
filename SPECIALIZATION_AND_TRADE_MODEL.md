# Specialization and Trade Model

## Core Design Principle
The game should use:

**symmetric mechanics, asymmetric flavor**

That means every player gets the same underlying development structure, but expressed through a different resource identity.

The important nuance is this:
**specialization defines what you are best at producing, not the full list of goods that exist in the game.**

### In plain English
- Every player advances through the same shape of progression
- No specialization should be inherently better just because of its tech tree
- The difference is theme, visuals, names, and the flavor of produced goods
- Players choose a primary production identity, not a superior class
- The economy contains a larger shared pool of goods than any one player can cover alone

---

## Why This Matters
This solves several problems at once.

### 1. Balance stays sane
If every resource gets a wildly unique tech tree, balancing becomes miserable.
A shared template keeps things understandable.

### 2. Players still feel distinct
Even if the structure is shared, Timber should feel different from Stone or Grain because the expression is different.

### 3. Trade makes more sense
If players occupy equivalent but differently flavored production roles, they can exchange goods without one specialization becoming the obvious best pick.

### 4. The game feels fair
A player should not feel punished for picking the "wrong" starting resource.

---

## Shared Global Goods Pool
The world should contain one large catalog of goods.

Examples of broad categories:
- raw materials
- construction materials
- food goods
- tools / industrial goods
- civic / service goods
- luxury / prestige goods

Long-term, this catalog can become large.
It could eventually grow to dozens or even 100+ goods.

But the key design rule is:
- **the world has one shared goods language**
- **players specialize in producing only part of it efficiently**

That creates real supply-chain gameplay instead of isolated personal tech trees.

---

## Shared Advancement Template
Each specialization should map onto the same abstract ladder.

### Tier roles
1. **Raw extraction**
2. **Refined processing**
3. **Advanced production**
4. **Prestige / luxury / export**

Each specialization should roughly match in:
- build cost curve
- maintenance curve
- output band
- upgrade cadence
- strategic usefulness

The names and presentation change. The skeleton does not.

## Important Balance Rule
Do not let flavor drift become hidden mechanical imbalance.

Things to watch:
- one tree becoming easier to understand than the others
- one chain becoming a mandatory bottleneck for everyone else
- one specialization having better margins at every tier
- one theme having clearer building readability or easier placement advantages

The test is simple:

**different skin, same skeleton**

---

## Development Through Trade
Players should sometimes need resources or goods they do not personally produce efficiently.
That is where trade becomes part of progression rather than just optional flavor.

The goal is not for every player to be completely self-contained.
The goal is for every player to participate in the same goods web from a different angle.

## Desired feeling
A player should be able to say:
- "I can grow on my own for a while"
- "but to advance efficiently, I need things other people make"

That creates healthy interdependence.

---

## Three Acquisition Channels
Goods should come from three sources:

### 1. Self-production
What your specialization and district are best at producing.

### 2. Player trade
Goods obtained from teammates, neighbors, or other players.

### 3. External city trade
Goods bought from or sold to outside cities/NPC trade partners.

These channels should work together.
A player is not locked to only one of them.

---

## Two Trade Layers

## 1. Player-to-player trade
This is the social/economic heart of multiplayer.

### Purpose
- get needed development inputs
- convert surplus into missing goods
- create specialization interdependence
- reward cooperation

### Example
- Timber player needs Masonry or Flour for district upgrades
- Stone player needs Wood Planks for advanced workshops
- Grain player needs Tools or Pottery for storage/market progression

This makes development feel connected instead of isolated.

### Design rule
Player trade should help you advance faster, more smoothly, or into better upgrades.
It should not feel like pointless bartering for tiny bonuses.

---

## 2. External city trade
This is the Pharaoh-like trade layer.

These are not real players. They are outside trade partners or foreign cities.

### Purpose
- provide a broader market
- create buy/sell opportunities beyond the current active player base
- act as resource sinks and sources
- make the world feel larger than the current match/population

### How it should feel
More like Pharaoh than like an auction house.

Meaning:
- outside cities have distinct demands
- they only buy/sell certain categories of goods
- trade routes can open, close, or have limits
- some goods are profitable exports, others are scarce imports
- players configure trade relationships rather than instantly materializing goods from nowhere

### Good uses
- export surplus goods for income
- import goods when local player supply is weak
- create medium-term planning around routes and demand

---

## Relationship Between the Two Trade Layers
These two systems should support each other, not compete.

### Player trade should be best for
- direct cooperation
- quick fulfillment of development bottlenecks
- specialization synergy
- social economy

### External city trade should be best for
- broader market stability
- converting surplus into income
- filling gaps when player supply is weak
- creating world-level economic texture

---

## Progression Dependency Recommendation
To make trade matter, some advancements should require access to goods outside your primary production lane.

That does **not** mean each building has one fixed universally imported ingredient forever.
It means progression depends on what you already produce versus what you do not.

A good pattern is:
- your own specialization covers part of the requirement efficiently
- the rest comes from the broader goods pool
- those missing goods can be sourced from players or external trade

### Example pattern
A Tier 3 or Tier 4 upgrade might require:
- one or more core goods from your own specialization
- one construction or tooling good from elsewhere
- one food, service, or prestige input from elsewhere

This creates a reason to:
- cooperate with players
- buy from external cities
- build trade infrastructure

Without this, everyone will tend to self-isolate.

---

## Design Guardrail
Do not make players completely helpless without trade.
That becomes frustrating.

Better model:
- self-sufficiency is possible but slower or less efficient
- trade accelerates development and unlocks stronger pathways

That keeps multiplayer meaningful without making solo moments miserable.

---

## Recommended V1 Goods Scope
The long-term model can support a very large goods catalog.
But v1 should be much smaller.

### Recommendation
Start with roughly **12-20 goods**, not 100.

### Suggested v1 categories
- **Raw:** timber, stone, grain, clay, iron ore
- **Construction:** wood planks, bricks, cut stone
- **Food:** flour, bread
- **Industrial:** iron bars, tools
- **Civic/Luxury:** pottery, furniture, fine goods

This is enough to prove:
- specialization
- trade dependency
- external imports/exports
- multi-step development

without making the game unreadable.

---

## Summary
The intended model is:

- every specialization uses the same progression skeleton
- each specialization expresses that skeleton through a unique resource theme
- the world runs on one broader shared goods pool
- players have a primary production identity, not total exclusivity
- player-to-player trade supports cooperative advancement
- external city trade provides Pharaoh-style world economy pressure and opportunity

That gives us fairness, identity, interdependence, and a real reason for multiplayer economics to exist.
